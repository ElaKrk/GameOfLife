Game of life

About game of life

The Game of Life also known simply as Life, is a "cellular automaton" devised by John Horton Conway, the British mathematician in 1970.

John Horton Conway was born in 1937. He is known for the invention of the Game of Life, but he contributed to many areas of science such as: the theory of finite groups, number theory, combinatorial game theory, coding theory etc. He received various of prizes for his work:

Conway received the Berwick Prize (1971), was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society (1981), was the first recipient of the Pólya Prize (LMS) (1987), won the Nemmers Prize in Mathematics (1998) and received the Leroy P. Steele Prize for Mathematical Exposition (2000) of the American Mathematical Society.

The game shows that the evolution is determined by its initial state. You can interact with the Game of Life by creating an initial configuration. It was publicly presented in 1970 in Scientific American magazine. Since then it became very popular thanks to surprising ways in which patterns can evolve. Ultimately it helped launch a new branch of mathematics: a field of cellular automata.

Rules of the GAME

Rules:

  • each cell can be in one of two states: alive or dead
  • every cell has eight neighbours, which are the cells that are horizontally, vertically or diagonally adjacent
  • live cells with fewer than two live neighbours dies, caused by underpopulation
  • live cells with two or three live neighbours lives on to the next generation
  • live cells with more than three live neighbours dies, by overpopulation
  • dead cells with exactly three live neighbours becomes alive cell thanks to reproduction
